Introduction

Insurance companies are always looking for ways and means to identify fraudulent claims payout and keep premiums low for customers. They crunch data and analyze it statistically from time immemorial to find out fatality rates, adjust their premiums and so on. Fortunately for them there are several tools and languages available today that can help them analyze data to predict or arrive at a result proving or disproving their hypothesis.

My focus today is in health insurance claims and its correlation with parameters (called features in data science) such as age, body mass index (BMI) whether an individual is a smoker or not and so on. It is a common perception that health insurance claim amounts are directly proportional to age and BMI. That is, the higher the age of the individual or their BMI the higher the claim amount. Looked at another way is it inversely proportional to the physical activity (steps walked per day) undertaken by that individual? Insurers or their actuaries pose such questions constantly to themselves and are in a perpetual quest to find answers that can improve customer service and their profitability.

To find out some answers I took a sample data from Kaggle: https://www.kaggle.com/easonlai/sample-insurance-claim-prediction-dataset and analyzed it to verify my hypothesis.

Hypothesis and Dataset

My hypothesis is that while there could be some correlation between age and health insurance claim amount, no such correlation exists with BMI and claim amount. I also think that increased physical activities will reduce claims that will help insurance companies to keep premiums low.

Let us analyze the data and find out. My dataset is small and has 1338 records consisting of the following columns.

age: age of policyholder
sex: gender of policy holder (female=0, male=1)
bmi: Body mass index, providing an understanding of body, weights that are relatively high or low relative to height,
    objective index of body weight (kg / m ^ 2) using the ratio of height to weight, ideally 18.5 to 25
steps: average walking steps per day of policyholder
children: number of children / dependents of policyholder
smoker: smoking state of policyholder (non-smoke=0;smoker=1)
region: the residential area of policyholder in the US (northeast=0, northwest=1, southeast=2, southwest=3)
charges: individual medical costs billed by health insurance
insuranceclaim: yes=1, no=0

Let us look at the data we have and see the relation between the various parameters collected in this dataset.

We plot the "charges" feature with other features such as "age", "bmi", "steps" (physical activity), and "smoker". Where the x and y axes values are continuous, we use a scatter plot. When "charges" is plotted against a categorical data such as "sex" we use a bar graph using the median of "charges".

A word of caution. Using the plotted values of certain pairs of features, one cannot, in general, come to a firm conclusion. For example, though higher age (senior citizens) may mean higher claims, we cannot conclude that the reverse it true. That is, younger people may or may not have a lower claim charge.

Thus, while the plots and bar graphs may show a trend or tendency we will have to build a machine learning algorithm eventually to further corroborate our hypothesis.

Further, I plan to build a simple web interface where users could input certain features like charges, age, BMI, Smoker or Non-Smoker and using machine learning algorithms behind the scene, the web interface will produce an output the probability of a higher claim charge.

Relation between Age vs. Claim Charges

Next, we plot the claim amounts ("charges") against age. Before we plot, we change the y-scale to range from 1,000 to 50,000 and the x-scale to vary from 10 to 70. We used these scale ranges based on the minimum and maximum values of the age and charges as outputted by the .describe dataframe method. See above.

While the actual data is a scattered plot, we also used a best-fit curve to show the relation between age and charges.

The data points themselves are in green color (c='g' parameter below) while the best fit curve is in red to differentiate it visually.
